CARUSO#2

Source: Enrico Caruso
One breath, one vocal posture, many rapidly changing pitches. Start as slowly as possible — precision first, speed later.

Rhythm

The exercise has four movements, all under one long slur:

Move Direction Rhythm Feel
1 Up 1 & a 2 & a 3 & a 4 e & a Triplet sixteenth groups
2 Down 1 & a 2 & a 3 & a 4 e & a Same triplet pattern, reversed
3 Up 1 & a 2 & a 3 & a 4 e & a Same triplet pattern again
4 Down straight 16ths Overlapping 4-note descending groups

Movement 4 — the 16th note descent — works like this: each group of 4 descends, then the next group starts back up on the 3rd note of the previous group, creating an overlap:

C B A G  →  A G F E  →  F E D C  →  ...

Each new group begins two steps above where the last group landed — the chain descends by overlapping 4-note figures, not in one straight drop.

What_It_Does

  • Ascending by small scale fragments — overlapping three- and four-note groups, not a simple scalar run.
  • Changing rhythmic subdivision — alternates between triplet and straight sixteenth figures.
  • Large slurs — long slurs spanning many notes. One continuous breath, one vocal gesture — not separate notes.
  • Crosses the passaggio without resetting — changing rhythms and slurs keep the larynx and resonance coordinated as pitch changes rapidly.
  • Develops agility, not range — trains the ability to change pitch rapidly while keeping tone even.

Classification

Melismatic agility exercise / coloratura study.

Fundamentally different from CARUSO#1 — where #1 is a scalar warm-up for registration and resonance, #2 trains agility: the rapid pitch changes, mixed rhythms, and long slurs force the voice to stay coordinated rather than re-setting between notes.

How_To_Practice

Sing very slowly first. That seems counterintuitive for an agility exercise, but slowness lets you calibrate every motor movement with accuracy before increasing tempo. Once the coordination is precise at slow speed, tempo increases without breaking the pattern. This is consistent with modern motor learning theory — and exactly what Caruso's caption describes.
